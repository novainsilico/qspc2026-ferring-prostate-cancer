Title: A Mechanistic-Statistical QSP framework of Disease Progression and Clinical Outcomes in Prostate Cancer

Authors: 
Matthias Hanke(1), Théo Plissonneau(1), Adèle Hamelin(1), Philippe Pinton(2), Maria Deryabina(2), Lars-Erik Kyhl(2), Simon Bjerregaard(2), Jonas Eildal(2), Johan Kjellstroem(2), Cristhyne Leon(1)
1 Nova In Silico, France
2 Ferring Pharmaceuticals, Denmark

Background: Prostate cancer is the second most diagnosed malignancy in men and a leading cause of cancer-related mortality. Localized disease is often managed with radical prostatectomy and/or radiotherapy, 
but many patients experience recurrence and require androgen deprivation therapy (ADT). We present a quantitative systems pharmacology (QSP) mechanistic–statistical framework that links pharmacodynamic (PD) drivers—namely testosterone suppression 
under different ADT schedules—to disease progression from hormone-sensitive prostate cancer to metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer and to clinically relevant endpoints such as time to metastasis and prostate-cancer–specific mortality.

Methods: We developed an integrative framework connecting mechanistic representations of disease biology to clinical outcomes. Its core is a mechanistic disease model describing prostate cancer cell proliferation and prostate-specific antigen (PSA) 
production as functions of testosterone levels and downstream androgen receptor signaling. Adapted from Jain et al. [1], the model was calibrated using preclinical data from animal models [3,4], as well as clinical data from patients receiving continuous 
or intermittent ADT [4]. The model captures adaptive disease dynamics underlying the transition from hormone-sensitive to castration-resistant states.
This model is coupled with a statistical time-to-event model representing the bottleneck for metastasis formation in patients with localized hormone-sensitive prostate cancer. This component, adapted from Szczurek et al. [5], 
was extended to incorporate disease-specific covariates, including baseline PSA and first-line treatment. Calibration leveraged individual-level SEER data [6] and summary-level clinical data [7] to quantify the relationship 
between model-predicted PSA response and clinical endpoints.

Results: The integrated QSP framework reproduces a broad range of preclinical and clinical observations. By combining patient-specific covariates — such as age, Gleason score, disease stage, baseline PSA, and first-line treatment — 
with simulated PSA dynamics, the model predicts time to metastasis and prostate-cancer–specific mortality. Simulations characterize disease progression under alternative ADT schedules and degrees of testosterone suppression, 
demonstrating improved outcomes with continuous versus intermittent ADT and with deep castration (testosterone <20 ng/mL) compared with non-deep castration (20–50 ng/mL).

Conclusion: This QSP framework enables in silico evaluation of ADT strategies across preclinical and clinical contexts, providing mechanistic insight into how testosterone modulation influences disease progression and outcomes. 
By integrating preclinical PD data with clinical disease and survival models, clinically relevant hypotheses can be explored even when investigational therapies remain at an early, preclinical stage, although prediction of 
long-term outcomes may require further clinical validation.
